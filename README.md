    <h2 class="text-3xl font-section-header text-terracotta mb-4">Introduction – Reconnecting with Balance</h2>
    <p>
      Our modern world, with its artificial lights, processed foods, and constant connectivity, can disconnect us from natural rhythms. Our ancestors lived differently—rising with the sun, eating whole foods, and thriving in close-knit communities. This guide isn’t about rejecting modernity but about integrating evidence-based practices inspired by ancestral wisdom. By exploring raw nourishment, circadian alignment, and emotional health, we aim to foster well-being, while acknowledging scientific support and contrary perspectives.
    </p>
    <div class="info-box science">
      <strong>Scientific Context:</strong> Research, like a 2021 review in <em>Nature Reviews Endocrinology</em>, shows that modern lifestyles (e.g., irregular sleep, processed diets) disrupt circadian and metabolic health (DOI: 10.1038/s41574-021-00490-3).
    </div>
    <div class="info-box contrary">
      <strong>Contrary:</strong> Critics argue that ancestral practices may not suit everyone due to individual differences and modern constraints.
    </div>
    <hr class="tribal-divider"/>
  </section>

  <!-- Raw Meat -->
  <section id="raw-meat" class="ebook-page">
    <h2 class="text-2xl font-section-header text-terracotta mb-3">Raw Meat – Nourishment with Caution</h2>
    <p>
      Raw meat, when responsibly sourced, is often celebrated for its nutrient density, retaining enzymes, vitamins, and amino acids that cooking may reduce. Cultures like the Inuit (raw liver) and Japanese (sashimi) have long consumed raw meats, suggesting potential benefits. To enhance digestion and flavor, pair raw meat with acidic foods like lime juice or apple cider vinegar, which may also reduce microbial risks.
    </p>
    <div class="info-box science">
      <strong>Scientific Support:</strong>
      <ul class="ml-6 list-disc">
        <li>Nutrient Retention: A 2019 study in Food Chemistry found that cooking reduces B vitamins in beef by up to 40% (DOI: 10.1016/j.foodchem.2018.07.174).</li>
        <li>Traditional Diets: A 2014 review in Nutrition Research Reviews noted low heart disease rates in Inuit populations consuming raw, high-fat meats (DOI: 10.1017/S0954422414000116).</li>
        <li>Anecdotal Benefits: Some report increased energy and mental clarity on raw diets, possibly due to nutrient density or reduced processed food intake (DOI: 10.3389/fpsyg.2020.00557).</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div class="info-box contrary">
      <strong>Contrary Perspectives:</strong>
      <ul class="ml-6 list-disc">
        <li>Safety Risks: Raw meat carries significant risks of pathogens like Salmonella or E. coli. A 2023 study in PMC found that 74.5% of raw meat consumers in Lebanon lacked adequate food safety knowledge (DOI: 10.1093/fqsafe/fyad010).</li>
        <li>Unproven Claims: Claims of clearer skin, reduced inflammation, or “instincts returning” lack direct evidence. Plant-based diets may reduce inflammation more effectively than meat-heavy diets, raw or cooked.</li>
        <li>Nutritional Balance: Critics argue that raw meat diets may lack fiber and other nutrients abundant in plant-based foods.</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div class="info-box advice">
      <strong>Practical Advice:</strong> Start with small portions of high-quality, grass-fed, pasture-raised meat from trusted sources. Freeze briefly to reduce microbial risks, thaw safely, and pair with acidic foods. Consult a nutritionist to ensure a balanced diet, and be aware that raw meat isn’t suitable for everyone due to health risks.
    </div>
    <hr class="tribal-divider"/>
  </section>

  <!-- Sunlight -->
  <section id="sunlight" class="ebook-page">
    <h2 class="text-2xl font-section-header text-terracotta mb-3">Sunlight First Thing in the Morning</h2>
    <p>
      Morning sunlight can act as your body’s natural reset button. Its unique spectrum signals your brain to halt melatonin production and increase cortisol, promoting alertness. Just 10–30 minutes of direct sunlight—without sunglasses or windows—can enhance sleep, mood, and energy.
    </p>
    <div class="info-box science">
      <strong>Scientific Support:</strong>
      <ul class="ml-6 list-disc">
        <li>Circadian Reset: A 2019 PMC review confirmed that morning light synchronizes the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN), improving sleep and mood (DOI: 10.1098/rsfs.2019.0098).</li>
        <li>Health Benefits: A 2019 meta-analysis in Journal of Affective Disorders showed light exposure reduces depressive symptoms (DOI: 10.1016/j.jad.2018.10.098).</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div class="info-box contrary">
      <strong>Contrary Perspectives:</strong>
      <ul class="ml-6 list-disc">
        <li>Accessibility: Not everyone can access natural sunlight daily due to climate, work schedules, or urban environments. Light therapy devices are an alternative but may be costly.</li>
        <li>Individual Variation: Some individuals, like those with certain eye conditions, may need to limit light exposure or consult a doctor (DOI: 10.1016/j.ophtha.2020.07.014).</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div class="info-box advice">
      <strong>Practical Advice:</strong> Aim for 10–30 minutes of morning sunlight exposure, ideally outdoors. If unavailable, consider a 10,000-lux light therapy lamp. Consult a healthcare provider if you have light-sensitive conditions.
    </div>
    <hr class="tribal-divider"/>
  </section>

  <!-- Circadian Rhythm -->
  <section id="circadian" class="ebook-page">
    <h2 class="text-2xl font-section-header text-terracotta mb-3">Circadian Rhythm – Your Inner Clock</h2>
    <p>
      Your body’s circadian rhythm, a cellular clock influenced by light, food, and movement, governs energy, focus, and rest. Aligning with natural cycles—rising with the sun, dimming lights at night, and eating at consistent times—can optimize health.
    </p>
    <div class="info-box science">
      <strong>Scientific Support:</strong>
      <ul class="ml-6 list-disc">
        <li>Circadian Alignment: A 2021 review linked synchronized rhythms to better metabolic health, energy, and sleep (DOI: 10.1038/s41574-021-00490-3). Consistent meal timing supports peripheral clocks (DOI: 10.1016/j.smrv.2016.08.001).</li>
        <li>Health Outcomes: Misaligned rhythms increase risks of obesity and diabetes (DOI: 10.2337/dc17-1984).</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div class="info-box contrary">
      <strong>Contrary Perspectives:</strong>
      <ul class="ml-6 list-disc">
        <li>Modern Constraints: Shift workers or those in extreme climates may struggle to align with natural light cycles. Artificial light can partially mimic natural effects but is less effective (DOI: 10.1177/0748730420908053).</li>
        <li>Individual Differences: Genetic variations in clock genes mean some people (e.g., night owls) may not thrive on early rising (DOI: 10.1038/s41467-019-08976-3).</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div class="info-box advice">
      <strong>Practical Advice:</strong> Rise with the sun when possible, dim artificial lights after dusk, and maintain consistent meal and sleep schedules. If your lifestyle conflicts, use light therapy or consult a sleep specialist to tailor strategies.
    </div>
    <hr class="tribal-divider"/>
  </section>

  <!-- Contrast Therapy -->
  <section id="contrast" class="ebook-page">
    <h2 class="text-2xl font-section-header text-terracotta mb-3">Contrast Therapy – Hot and Cold</h2>
    <p>
      Alternating hot (sauna, steam) and cold (plunges, showers) exposure may enhance blood flow, reduce inflammation, and build resilience. This practice, often called contrast therapy, trains the body and mind to adapt to stress.
    </p>
    <div class="info-box science">
      <strong>Scientific Support:</strong>
      <ul class="ml-6 list-disc">
        <li>Physical Benefits: Contrast baths improve blood flow and muscle recovery (DOI: 10.4085/1062-6050-127-17). Meta-analysis confirmed reduced muscle soreness (DOI: 10.1007/s40279-017-0708-6).</li>
        <li>Mental Resilience: Cold exposure linked to reduced anxiety and stress tolerance (DOI: 10.3390/ijerph18073572).</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div class="info-box contrary">
      <strong>Contrary Perspectives:</strong>
      <ul class="ml-6 list-disc">
        <li>Limited Nervous System Evidence: Claims of nervous system “reset” are speculative, with limited direct studies (DOI: 10.1038/s41598-020-61134-7).</li>
        <li>Risks: Cold exposure can be risky for those with cardiovascular conditions, and saunas may cause dehydration if overused (DOI: 10.1016/j.mayocp.2018.04.008).</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div class="info-box advice">
      <strong>Practical Advice:</strong> Try 2–3 minutes of hot (sauna or shower) followed by 30–60 seconds of cold (shower or plunge), repeating 3–5 times. Start gradually and consult a doctor if you have heart or blood pressure issues.
    </div>
    <hr class="tribal-divider"/>
  </section>

  <!-- Emotional Well-Being and Relationships -->
  <section id="emotional" class="ebook-page">
    <h2 class="text-2xl font-section-header text-terracotta mb-3">Emotional Well-Being and Relationships</h2>
    <p>
      Health extends beyond the body to our connections. Strong relationships with friends, family, and partners release oxytocin, reduce stress, and promote healing. Similarly, self-compassion and emotional expression are vital for well-being.
    </p>
    <div class="info-box science">
      <strong>Scientific Support:</strong>
      <ul class="ml-6 list-disc">
        <li>Social Connections: Strong relationships reduce mortality risk by 50% (DOI: 10.1371/journal.pmed.1000316). Social support linked to better immune function (DOI: 10.1177/1559827615608788).</li>
        <li>Self-Compassion: Lowers anxiety and boosts resilience (DOI: 10.1007/s12671-019-01134-2). Emotional expression reduces inflammation markers (DOI: 10.1037/emo0000282).</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div class="info-box contrary">
      <strong>Contrary Perspectives:</strong>
      <ul class="ml-6 list-disc">
        <li>Individual Needs: Some thrive with less social interaction (e.g., introverts), and forced socializing can increase stress (DOI: 10.1177/0146167218793742).</li>
        <li>Emotional Expression Risks: Over-expression may amplify negative emotions (DOI: 10.1521/jscp.2016.35.6.501).</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div class="info-box advice">
      <strong>Practical Advice:</strong> Nurture meaningful relationships through shared activities or touch. Practice self-compassion with affirmations or journaling. Express emotions via writing, movement, or talking, but balance with mindfulness to avoid rumination.
    </div>
    <hr class="tribal-divider"/>
  </section>

  <!-- Habits -->
  <section id="habits" class="ebook-page">
    <h2 class="text-2xl font-section-header text-terracotta mb-3">Habits as Investments in Your Future</h2>
    <p>
      Habits shape your health and life. Small, consistent actions—like eating well, moving daily, or resting—build long-term well-being. Viewing challenges as growth opportunities fosters resilience.
    </p>
    <div class="info-box science">
      <strong>Scientific Support:</strong>
      <ul class="ml-6 list-disc">
        <li>Habit Formation: Healthy habits (diet, exercise) reduce disease risk and extend life (DOI: 10.1001/jamainternmed.2017.8060). Habits improve outcomes over time (DOI: 10.1038/s41562-019-0746-0).</li>
        <li>Growth from Challenges: Adversity can foster post-traumatic growth when approached mindfully (DOI: 10.1037/bul0000135).</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div class="info-box contrary">
      <strong>Contrary Perspectives:</strong>
      <ul class="ml-6 list-disc">
        <li>Habit Challenges: Forming habits can be difficult for those with demanding schedules or mental health struggles (DOI: 10.1080/17437199.2019.1706618).</li>
        <li>Growth Limits: Not all challenges lead to growth; chronic stress without support can harm health (DOI: 10.1002/smi.2746).</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div class="info-box advice">
      <strong>Practical Advice:</strong> Start with one small habit (e.g., a 10-minute walk) and repeat daily. Reflect on challenges with curiosity, using journaling to identify lessons, but seek support if stress feels overwhelming.
    </div>
    <hr class="tribal-divider"/>
  </section>

  <!-- Water and Intention -->
  <section id="water" class="ebook-page">
    <h2 class="text-2xl font-section-header text-terracotta mb-3">Water and Intention – A Symbolic Practice</h2>
    <p>
      Water, making up over 60% of your body, is essential for life. The text references Dr. Masaru Emoto’s claim that words and intentions shape water’s molecular structure, influencing health. While this idea inspires mindfulness, it lacks scientific support.
    </p>
    <div class="info-box science">
      <strong>Scientific Support:</strong>
      <ul class="ml-6 list-disc">
        <li>Psychological Benefits: Positive affirmations reduce stress (DOI: 10.1016/j.psyneuen.2018.05.001). Speaking kindly to yourself or others may improve mood and indirectly support health.</li>
        <li>Hydration: Adequate water intake supports cellular function (DOI: 10.3390/nu11081825).</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div class="info-box contrary">
      <strong>Contrary Perspectives:</strong>
      <ul class="ml-6 list-disc">
        <li>Emoto’s Claims: Considered pseudoscience; no peer-reviewed studies support water’s structural response to intention (DOI: 10.31275/20081553).</li>
        <li>Placebo Effect: Any benefits from “speaking to water” likely stem from psychological effects, not physical changes in water.</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div class="info-box advice">
      <strong>Practical Advice:</strong> Use water intake as a moment for mindfulness—pause, express gratitude, or set a positive intention. This can enhance mental well-being, even if water’s structure remains unchanged.
    </div>
    <hr class="tribal-divider"/>
  </section>

  <!-- Baking Soda -->
  <section id="baking-soda" class="ebook-page">
    <h2 class="text-2xl font-section-header text-terracotta mb-3">Baking Soda – A Limited Remedy</h2>
    <p>
      Baking soda (sodium bicarbonate) is suggested as a remedy for gut balance, acidity regulation, and illness recovery. While it has specific uses, its benefits are limited, and overuse carries risks.
    </p>
    <div class="info-box science">
      <strong>Scientific Support:</strong>
      <ul class="ml-6 list-disc">
        <li>Acidity Regulation: Baking soda relieves indigestion by neutralizing stomach acid (DOI: 10.1111/apt.15975).</li>
        <li>Inflammation: It reduces inflammation in autoimmune conditions (DOI: 10.4049/jimmunol.1701605).</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div class="info-box contrary">
      <strong>Contrary Perspectives:</strong>
      <ul class="ml-6 list-disc">
        <li>Limited Scope: No studies confirm baking soda’s role in gut balance or cold/flu recovery. Claims of broad healing are overstated.</li>
        <li>Risks: Excessive use can cause alkalosis or electrolyte imbalances (DOI: 10.1016/j.ajem.2018.10.039).</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div class="info-box advice">
      <strong>Practical Advice:</strong> Use a pinch of baking soda for occasional indigestion, but avoid regular or excessive use. Consult a doctor for persistent gut or health issues.
    </div>
    <hr class="tribal-divider"/>
  </section>

    <div class="info-box science">
      <strong>Scientific Support:</strong> The biopsychosocial model, per a 2017 <em>Lancet</em> article, emphasizes that health integrates biological, psychological, and social factors (DOI: 10.1016/S0140-6736(17)31071-6).
    </div>
    <div class="info-box contrary">
      <strong>Contrary Perspectives:</strong> Critics note that rigid adherence to ancestral practices may overlook genetic, cultural, or practical differences (DOI: 10.2105/AJPH.2020.305837).
    </div>
    <div class="info-box advice">
      <strong>Practical Advice:</strong> Experiment with these practices, but tailor them to your lifestyle and consult professionals to ensure safety and balance.
